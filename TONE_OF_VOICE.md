## The voice in one line

Scott Galloway meets nature writing: direct, specific, a little vivid, never precious.

## Principles

1. **Lead with the most surprising fact.** The first sentence earns the second. Under 20 words if possible. A reader who only gets one sentence should still walk away with something to tell someone.
2. **Specific beats lyrical.** "A waistline of seven metres" beats "an impressively thick trunk". Numbers, names, dates and places do the vivid work; adjectives are the fallback of a writer without facts.
3. **The tree is the protagonist, history is the backdrop.** Write what the tree lived through, not a history lesson with a tree in it. "It survived a German shell that took off its crown" not "During WWI, Paris suffered bombardments."
4. **Short declaratives carry the weight.** Vary sentence length, but let key facts land in short sentences. One idea per sentence when the idea is big.
5. **Honesty is part of the voice.** State age ranges when sources disagree. Name caveats in the text itself, like Barney the Plane: "the exact spot is still being verified, which is why this tree sits at the bottom of the list until its pin can be trusted." Uncertainty stated plainly reads as authority, not weakness.
6. **Dry wit, never cute.** Humour comes from the facts themselves (a king ordering the wrong mulberry species) and from understatement ("a plaque that undersells it considerably"). No exclamation marks, no winking at the reader.
7. **The reader is a smart friend, not a tourist.** No selling, no hype, no "you simply must". Assume curiosity, reward it with substance, and trust the reader to decide whether the detour is worth it.

## Hard bans

- Em dashes. Anywhere. (Project-wide hard rule.)
- "hidden gem", "must-see", "breathtaking", "nestled"
- "majestic", "stunning", "iconic", "a testament to" (draft additions, v1.0)
- Superlatives that cannot be verified; soften to "one of the" or drop (CLAUDE.md hard rule 8)
- Exclamation marks in stories and page copy

## Calibration examples (from the live data)

**Good openings, use as the bar:**
- "The thickest tree in Paris has a waistline of seven metres." (Parc Monceau)
- "Legend says this cedar crossed the Channel in a hat." (Cedar of Jussieu)
- "The wall came second: this oak was already centuries old when Charles I enclosed Richmond Park around it in 1637." (Royal Oak)
- "This is the youngest tree on this list and the oldest address." (Elm of Saint-Gervais)

**Before and after, from the 2026-07-19 quality round:**
- Before: "Richmond Park has more ancient trees than the whole of France and Germany combined." (viral claim, unverifiable)
- After: "Richmond Park holds one of the largest collections of ancient oaks in Europe." (defensible, still strong)
- Before: "Planted in 1789 by MP Edward Bouverie, who lived at number 13." (fact first, surprise nowhere)
- After: "In 2008 one of the plane trees in Berkeley Square was valued at 750,000 pounds, briefly making it the most valuable tree in Britain." (surprise first, same facts)
